# Elements of LitRPG

It's unlikely that you'll use all of these elements in every game, although
you could. The LitRPG genre is already a bit of what has been called "gonzo"
since the early days of TTRPGs. It mixes genres by definition because the
LitRPG game elements make certain elements that aren't normally supposed
to be known to the characters visible to them.

## Stats/Stat Blocks

This is simply what we all know for RPGs.  These fall into two categories.
First, there are our character sheets.  There is a very important distinction
when playing a LitRPG-based game.  The characters themselves are aware that
they are characters, with character sheets.  They know that they are in a game
with game mechanics.  They may or may not be able to read their own character
sheets.

The other category is the stat blocks of everything else.  That includes
monsters, items, vehicles, etc.  It's possible that characters will be able to
explicitly see these, but probably not.  If they are, ot should be through
various Detect abilities that provide a limited window into the full stat
block.  However, unlike a regular RPG setting, it's possible that those Detect
abilities will state what they detect explicitly in game terms.

## Skills

The only difference between this and skills in any RPG is that some of the
skills may be skills regarding interactions with the game system itself.  This
is a couple of meta-layers deep.  In written LitRPG, there's never a stated
skill level at using the game system, but there are skills in using it that
are described qualitatively.  Characters learn interact with the inventory
system, the maps, the chat system and so forth.

Aside from that, skills work normally.  Just choose a skill list that's
relevent to the subgenre.  Some LitRPG novels make the skills very
fine-grained.  It's part of the fun of reading some of the more humorous
LitRPGs.

If the characters are entering the game from the real world, as they would for
isekai or system apocalypse games, they should start with real world skills,
not skills from the game, although prior experience with RPGs, either video or
tabletop can be treated as a skill with the interface.  For those subgenres of
LitRPG, it isn't necessary to be rigid about what real world skills are
available and how many each character can have.  Give them points, and some
suggestions.  They can select skills learned in childhood, school, including
university or even graduate degrees, professional, and hobbies.  Some things
to consider:

### Childhood skills
#### Any sports, particularly sports that require throwing something or fighting
#### Languages. If the interface doesn't translate, this may be useful

### School
#### Computing. This may help in hacking or circumventing the interface
#### Sciences
#### Art and music

### Professional
Get creative here
#### If the jobs requires a lot of driving, they can have vehicle skills
#### Math
#### Sketching
#### Police and military will probably have weapon skill levels
#### Medical professionals will have some first aid, and possibly diagnostic skills

### Hobbies
#### Gaming
#### Orienteering
#### Camping
#### Cooking
#### Collecting something that actually shows up in the setting


## Inventory

This can be handled in two ways.  Either the characters wear their armor,
wield their weapons and carry their loot and equipment just like in any RPG,
or they explicitly interact with an inventory system.  It depends on how
closely you want to adhere to the underlying genre of the RPG within the game
versus how much you want to make the game itself the setting.

One of the options is a hot list.  This could be modelled as a Naked Advantage
that can only be applied to a limited number of items or powers at a time.  In
particular, treat using most powers as requiring extra time and treat the hot
list as a Naked Advantage that is actually eliminating the Extra Time
Limitation.

## Powers/Spells

It doesn't matter whether you want to go with fantasy, science fiction,
superheroes or any combination.  Give the characters stuff they can do that us
normals can't.  A huge part of the fun of this genre is that it's explicitly
part of the game within the game.  The characters know that.  They're aware of
it.  Bring the mechanics out in the open.

So, where this interacts with the mechanics of the HERO System is in what
choices the characters have.  How much are they able to influence what's
available to them and how much is it just dropped on them by the game itself.
I'm a fan of player agency.  I hate overly railroaded games.  In LitRPG,
there's going to be some element of railroading.  It's part of making the game
explicit.  How much is a separate issue.  The opponents and challenges are
going to be dictated by the game, but often with options.  They should always
have some choice about which powers/spells they spend time trying to level up.

## Race

Often the characters aren't human.  Sometimes, they're given a choice of
races.  There will be trade-offs.  Make it explicit both in game terms and in
role playing terms.  Goblins and fairies may despise each other.  Vampires are
going to be vulnerable to sunlight.

## Heads Up Display

This is explicit access to several other items on this list.  It could be the
inventory, the chat system, or the map.

## Maps

There are two aspects of this.  The first is that the characters have access
to the same kinds of information that we've all put on maps for the entire
history of TTRPGs.  They know where the things they've already found are.

The other aspect is that there can be powers that are tied to the map.  The
map can automatically show you things that those powers detect.  Perhaps
certain things in the game will be explicitly shown on the map automatically.
For example, all safe rooms within a particular radius are shown.  Or a
distinction on the map of whether creatures seen are players, NPCs or monsters
and whether they are friendly, neutral or hostile.

## System Chat

Yes, characters can speak to each other and leave notes for each other in ways
that make sense within the game world.  However, they may also have access to
a chat system provided by the game.  The important aspects of this are who
they can choose to talk to and who else can see it.  This doesn't have to be
modelled as a power, but it can be.  You can use any time of communication
mechanism, including telepathy or illusions to convey sensory information.

## Audience

This is common in System Apocalypse LitRPG.  The game is live entertainment
for an audience.  If that's the case, you have to decide what the audience can
and can't see.  Are the safe rooms off limits?  Can they read the chat system?
Can they send loot drops to the characters?

## System AI

This is a central concept of System Apocalypse LitRPG.  The game is being run
by a system, often an AI.  The AI will tend to have at least some personality.
It has guidelines, limitations, and goals.  The game has rules beyond the game
mechanics.  The players are supposed to be doing something within it.  The
System AI has ways to push them along.  Most of those are simply game
elements such as monsters and loot.  Some are things the AI can do, such as
teleporting characters, immobilizing them, giving them loot or taking it away.
Some AIs are fixing bugs as they go, either in the system or the rules.
They'll announce changes, or not.  Give the AI some explicit powers.  They
will be extremely powerful relative to the PCs, but not necessarily
omnipotent.

## Boss Battles

This works just like boss battles in any RPG.  It's just that the characters,
the AI, the audience, etc. are all aware of it.

## Levels

This is just a shorthand for how powerful characters, monsters, powers,
skills, etc. are.  You can either use raw point values from the HERO System or
you can put them into ranges to be less specific about them.  This goes
hand-in-hand with how explicit the information that characters have about
their own character sheets and the monsters' stat blocks are.  If they can see
the raw numbers, you don't have to batch them into ranges.  It's worth
considering whether the audience, if there is one, has finer or coarser
detail.

## Safe Rooms

There may be areas where combat can't happen.  The characters may actually be
invulnerable there, or simply immune to BODY damage and drains.  At a minimum,
they shouldn't be able to die, unless the system kills them.  One of the
reasons for the system to kill them would be if they violate the safe room
restrictions by attacking in a safe room.

## Save Points/Respawns

This goes along with Time Loops to some extent, but it could be an interesting
game mechanic in itself.  Either the characters can't permanently die, or they
have additional lives and can respawn.  Those respawns could take them back to
the beginning, but that's hard to play.  An easier choice for game play is to
use explicit save points that are fairly frequent, or just have them respawn
exactly where they died some time after it happened.
