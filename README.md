# Introduction

This book started from my desire to create a campaign based on a LitRPG series
I was reading.  I realized at the time that the HERO System was a good fit for
the project for a number of reasons.  I took some time to consider whether
other systems were suitable, and although some were, I think the HERO System
actually hits a sweet spot, which I'll explain as I go along.  But first, let
me set the stage.

## What is LitRPG?

You can easily search online for various definitions and lists of books and
series to learn about LitRPG.  I'll give my working definition here.  It's a
story that explicitly incorporates elements of RPGs, either tabletop, video
game, or both, into the story itself.  These generally include actual RPG game
mechanics, stats, abilities, character progression, boss battles, etc.  The
degree to which these are explicitly part of the story varies, but they are at
least implicit in the way the story plays out, and may go as far as complete
dumps of stat blocks.

## Subgenres of LitRPG

LitRPG isn't neccessarily tied to any specific RPG, although popular RPGs have
influenced the mechanics and monsters used in many of them.  Nor is it tied to
a specific genre, although fantasy is the most common genre used.  In fact,
the subgenres of LitRPG aren't divided by the subgenres of the literature it's
based on.

### Progression

The power of the characters increases over the course of the story.  This
often includes any or all of the following:

#### Stat increases
#### New skills and increasing proficiency with existing skills
#### New powers, spells and items
#### Increasing level

The monsters increase in power as well, as do the hazards of the environment.

There are usually opportunities to do things that increase the character's
power.  Those can include practicing skills, killing monsters, collecting
loot, and crafting.  It isn't unusual for more than one, or even all of these
methods to be present, although there may be an emphasis on one of them that
sets the tone.  For example, a Farm Life or Cozy/Slice of Life story will tend
to emphasize practicing skills and crafting.  A Dungeon/Tower setting will
of tne emphasize killing monsters and collecting loot instead.

### Isekai

The characters are transported or reborn into a game world.  An important
distinction between this subgenre and the System Apocalypse subgenre is that
only the PCs are transported rather than some major portion of the population.
Most of the people they meet, even if they are plain vanilla humans, or the
world's equivalent, are native to the game world, having been born there.

### System Apocalypse

System Apocalypse is where either a game system is imposed on Earth or a large
portion of Earth's population is transported into a game system.  As the name
implies, it's apocalyptic.  The system may be threatening to kill everyone or
at least nearly everyone.  There's usually some way out promised, but it
requires playing the game.

### Cozy/Slice of Life

This is much more character driven.  It's about making a life in the setting.
It overlaps heavily with Cozy/Slice of Life fantasy or science fiction, but
includes game elements, such as building skills, crafting, and so forth.  To
truly be cozy, the focus is generally not on combat, but it doesn't have to be
completely excluded.  However, the combat should be driven by conflict in
creating or maintaining the cozy elements.

### Farm Life

This is often about an adventurer settling down to what's supposed to be a
quiet retirement.  Early RPGs often had explicit guidelines for how certain
classes would build a keep or castle, acquire followers and stop going out to
fight monsters.  This subgenre focuses on the specific path or not seeking
political power either.  It often overlaps with Romance and Cozy/Slice of
Life.

### Timeloop

In this subgenre, the main character is stuck in a time loop.  Typically, they
remember previous iterations of the loop and can change their decisions on
each iteration.  Sometimes, there's an explicit number of loops.  There's a
goal of getting out of the loop.  The conditions for the loop to restart can
vary.

### Virtual Reality/MMO

This one easy to understand for RPG players, but harder to explain to non-RPG
players.  The characters in the story are the players of the game.  Their
characters in the game aren't them.  They are characters, and the players are
aware of the fact that they are playing a game.  They may be trapped in the
game or they may enter and leave the game to achieve particular goals in the
story.  I consider Ready Player One to be an example of this, even if it isn't
the most explicitly LitRPG example.

### Dungeon Crawler/Tower Climber

This subgenre overlaps very heavily with Progression.  The main character or
party are moving through levels or a dungeon or tower with the opponents and
challenges getting more powerful and more difficult at each level.  These are
generally built from the same concepts as a traditional RPG megadungeon.

### Harem/Romance

Play it if you want to.  It works pretty well as a LitRPG written subgenre,
but it tends to be in combination with one or more of the other subgenres.  It
can fit in with Progression or Dungeon/Tower, but in written form often finds
itself in combination with some of the gentler subgenres like Farm Life or
Cozy/Slice of Life.

A word of warning on this subgenre is in order.  Many examples of this are
good examples of crossovers with other subgenres.  They tend of have explicit
sexual content to the extent that they can legitimately be called erotica.
Others steer more towards romance with the erotic elements kept more at a
PG-13 or soft R level.  When reading examples for inspiration, keep that in
mind.  However, you can use elements of them in a game setting even when the
players don't want explicit sexual content as an element of their game.  Just
know that when you read in this subgenre, a lot of it is explicit.

### Monster Core/Monster Evolution

The main characters are the monsters.  They are protecting themselves from the
intruders into their world.  Oddly, this doesn't have to be exclusive.  One of
the best known series in LitRPG, Dungeon Crawler Carl, manages to incorporate
making some of the NPCs into genuine ongoing characters.

One example of this idea that isn't LitRPG is John Scalzi's book Redshirts.
The NPCs realize that they're NPCs in someone else's story.  Part of why they
realize it is that they notice they have a high casualty rate.

### Dungeon Core

The main character is the dungeon, or perhaps more appropriately, whatever is
running the dungeon.  This works well with the System Apocalypse subgenre.
Even when the protagonists are more traditional player characters, the dungeon
or dungeon system AI can be and often is a character in its own right.  What
makes Dungeon Core distinct as a subgenre is when the dungeon system is the
protagonist.

### Tamer/Summoner

This subgenre has the protagist(s) gaining power by taming or summoning
animals or monsters.  The important part is that most of the power is in the
tamed or summoned creatures, or in the power of the summoner over them rather
than in the protagonists.

This subgenre is sometimes jokingly referred to as having pets, and it
certainly can.  The distinction is that the pets aren't the protagonists.  The
Dungeon Crawler Carl series isn't a Tamer/Summoner series.  There are a number
of pets present.  Some of them are explicitly characters who act
independently, with their own goals.
